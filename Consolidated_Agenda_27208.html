HISTORY/DISCUSSION 

 
Current Context 
In recent years, cities across Canada have seen a significant increase in the severity of the 
housing crisis. This trend reflects both local and national challenges, including systemic poverty, 
the impacts of mental illness, the prevalence of toxic drugs, rising housing costs, and a 
persistent shortage of housing units. 
 

 

6 

As of August 2025, an estimated 100 encampments were present across the city, with as many 
as 700 individuals residing in them. Encampments are outdoor areas where one or more people 
who are unhoused shelter or live together, often using tents or other temporary structures. 
 
Many encampments are quiet, peaceful, and self-regulating, but some pose significant risks to 
inhabitants, neighbours, infrastructure, and properties due to open fires, accumulation of 
flammable materials and refuse, human waste, noise, and unlawful behaviour. Between June 
2024 and June 2025, the City of Winnipeg (the “City”) received 972 encampment-related 311 
calls, reflecting growing public concern about both the well-being of those living in 
encampments and the impacts on public spaces and nearby communities. 
 
The City is an active partner in the Province of Manitoba’s Your Way Home: Manitoba’s Plan to 
End Chronic Homelessness, which emphasizes a trauma-informed and housing first approach. 
Balancing the City’s obligations under this strategy with community concerns about safety, 
health, and the condition of public spaces remains a complex and ongoing challenge. 
 
While long-term housing solutions are the most effective and equitable means of reducing 
encampments, the City must also maintain public trust and community stability. Your Way 
Home, launched in early 2025, has progressed slowly due to a shortage of available housing 
units. As a result, public parks, riverbanks, and other shared spaces remain occupied and, in 
some cases, severely degraded. 
 
Many Canadian cities, such as Hamilton, Halifax, and Kingston, have adopted approaches that 
balance supporting people who are unhoused with reducing the social and environmental 
impacts of encampments. Key elements of successful strategies elsewhere have included: 
 

▪

  Partnering with senior levels of government to rapidly increase housing supply; 

▪

  Adopting clear policies defining Council and citizen expectations; 

▪

  Developing encampment response protocols clarifying the roles and responsibilities of 

municipal departments and external partners; and 

▪

  Allocating resources to coordination, data collection, garbage removal, and enforcement. 

 
Increasing Housing Supply 
When Your Way Home was announced, the Province noted that Manitoba is experiencing a 
housing shortage due, in part, to a continual loss of affordable housing stock, with an estimated 
24,000 rental units under $750 lost to fires, demolition, or redevelopment over the past 30 
years.  
 
Since November 1, 2022, the City has invested over $150 million through the Housing 
Accelerator Fund, Affordable Housing Now, and related programs, to stimulate housing 
development. The Public Service monthly report to Executive Policy Committee on Housing 
Statistics for September 2025 noted that 3,841 City-funded housing units had been approved for 
development during that time. As a partner in Your Way Home, the City has also dedicated 
several public properties for supportive housing. However, most of the new units expected 
through these initiatives will not be completed until 2027 or later, leaving a near-term housing 
gap that may continue to be filled by encampments. 
 
The City provides several additional housing support services in the community and financial 
support to numerous community partners. Examples include providing annual support for End 

 

7 

Homelessness Winnipeg, funding 24 Hour Mobile Outreach, supporting 24/7 safe spaces and 
providing land or facilities at no cost to projects aimed at mitigating the housing crisis.  
 
Senior levels of government have greater capacity to mitigate Winnipeg’s ongoing shortage of 
affordable housing, but progress continues to be measured in years rather than months. It is 
expected that the prevalence of encampments as an alternative mode of shelter will persist 
through 2026.  
 
Council Direction 
On September 25, 2025, Council adopted a motion directing the Public Service to increase 
enforcement of existing by-laws as they relate to encampments. This decision seeks to balance 
care for vulnerable residents with the need to uphold community standards, protect public 
spaces, and maintain confidence in the City’s ability to maintain its property responsibly. 
 
Winnipeg’s Parks By-law No. 85/2009, for example, regulates camping, fires, littering, and 
noise—activities often associated with encampments. Previously, enforcement in encampments  
was largely complaint-driven, with a focus on voluntary compliance, harm reduction, and fire 
safety.  
 
Council’s motion provides a path forward that places clear limits on acceptable sheltering on 
public lands. A draft Encampment Policy (“the Policy”) is attached for review as Appendix A. 
 
Encampment Policy 
 
The Policy outlines the City’s approach to regulating encampments on public land. The Policy 
does not impact the Winnipeg Police Service (“WPS”) or the Winnipeg Fire and Paramedic 
Service (“WFPS”)’s response to calls for service. The Policy is meant to inform the City’s day-to-
day, non-emergency regulation of encampments moving forward. However, WPS and WFPS 
operations can draw from the same resources as those referenced in the Policy, and 
emergency calls for service may, therefore, impact the City’s ability to expeditiously complete 
non-emergent encampment work. 
 
The Policy follows the lead of municipalities like Kingston, Ontario where the City permits 
unhoused residents to shelter overnight on public land and requires that they disassemble 
lodgings after dawn to return sites to their intended daily use. This approach recognizes the 
severe damage to public lands and rivers caused by residents erecting structures, cutting trees 
in parks, disposing of garbage in waterways, accumulating combustibles and other items 
resulting in public health and safety hazards. The intent of the Policy is to clarify limits for public 
behaviour and to provide the City with a remedy for encampments that pose the above 
challenges.   
 
The Policy is grounded in several guiding principles, designed to ensure all City actions  
promote safety and order in public spaces. The following is a list of the guiding principles with 
an explanation for why they were included: 
 
Accountability and Transparency: Establishing consistent, transparent procedures for 
decision-making will ensure City actions are fair, legally defensible, and publicly accountable. 
Transparency reinforces trust among residents, partners, and people living in encampments, 
while accountability ensures resources are used effectively and in line with Council direction. 
 

 

8 

Health and Well-being: Encampments can involve conditions that jeopardize health—such as 
fire risk, unsanitary environments, and exploitation—and the City has a duty to mitigate these 
risks. Embedding this principle reinforces that health and wellness considerations guide all 
actions, whether through outreach, housing support, or enforcement. It also demonstrates a 
balanced and coordinated approach—showing that enforcement is not punitive but part of a 
broader effort to protect life, restore public spaces, and connect people to stable housing and 
care. 
 
Maintaining Public Wellbeing and Order: This principle reflects the City’s statutory 
responsibility under The City of Winnipeg Charter to promote safety, health, and orderly 
community life. It underscores the City’s duty to maintain safe and accessible public spaces for 
all residents. 
 
The Policy also requires that the Public Service develop an Encampment Response Protocol 
(the “Protocol”) under the authority of the Chief Administrative Officer to be shared with Council 
as information, with outcomes to be reported annually. Where the Policy clarifies Council 
expectations for the City’s regulation of encampments in public spaces, the Protocol, attached 
as Appendix B, is an operational guide for the Public Service, defining how encampments will 
be prioritized and clarifying internal roles. 
 
Encampment Response Protocol 
In 2022, the Public Service adopted a Non-Emergent Encampment Support Process. This 
blueprint, though useful, was not strictly adhered to due to a lack of centralized coordination and 
a rapidly evolving encampment reality.  
 
The Protocol will enhance the Non-Emergent Encampment Support Process by incorporating 
Council’s direction, as articulated in the Policy. It will outline step-by-step procedures for 
identifying, prioritizing, and responding to encampments while maintaining alignment with the 
Policy’s guiding principles. 
 
Key features of the Protocol include: 
 

•  Encampment Coordination Table:

 It is essential that all relevant City departments 

have clear direction with respect to encampments. The Community Development 
Division will lead an Encampment Coordination Table responsible for triaging 
encampment-related requests, setting priorities, and coordinating appropriate responses 
across departments. 

 

•  Standardized Procedures: Defined processes and expectations for outreach, 

notification, enforcement, and remediation to ensure consistent practice and 
documentation. 

 

•  Prioritization Criteria: A transparent framework to assess encampments based on 

health, safety, environmental, and community impact factors. 

 

•  Clean-up Capacity: Dedicated sanitation and restoration resources are important to 

maintaining the safety and accessibility of public spaces once encampments are 

 

9 

vacated. City crews manage debris removal, waste disposal, hazardous materials, and 
site restoration under challenging conditions. Continued allocation and flexibility of 
resources is required for staffing, disposal, and remediation supplies to ensure sites can 
be restored quickly, safely, and sustainably.  
 

•  Property and Site Management: Procedures for the safe handling of personal property, 

waste removal, and site restoration will be developed within the Protocol. 

 
Establishing the Protocol ensures that City actions are coordinated and consistent. This 
Protocol will provide clarity for the Public Service and the public, reduce duplication across 
departments, and build confidence in the City’s efforts to address encampments and maintain 
safe and accessible public spaces. 
Conclusion 
If the recommendations of this report are approved, the Public Service will adopt the Policy as a 
guide and further develop the Protocol to meet Council expectations for managing 
encampments in public spaces. The Protocol will be regularly updated based on operational 
experience and other lessons learned. An annual progress report will be provided to Council, 
starting in January 2027.

1.  Purpose:  

 

The Encampments Policy (the “Policy”) outlines the City of Winnipeg’s (the “City”) approach 
to regulating Encampments on publicly owned land.   

 

2.  Definitions:  

 

2.1 “Garbage” includes: a) animal, human, vegetable and agricultural wastes, including 

manure; (b) ashes; (c) large items of refuse, including appliances, indoor furniture, 
dilapidated outdoor furniture, mattresses, large auto parts and crates; (d) 
construction and demolition wastes; (e) dead animals; (f) industrial refuse; (g) 
rubbish; (h) abandoned or unattended shopping carts; (i) hazardous wastes; (j) 
wrecked, dismantled, partially dismantled, inoperative, discarded, or abandoned 
trailers, machinery or vehicles or parts thereof; and (k) any deserted or discarded 
article, product or goods of manufacture; 
 

2.2 “Health and Safety Risk” means the conditions under which an Encampment 

presents a clear and imminent danger to the life, health, or safety of Encampment 
residents or the public. This includes Encampments located in flood zones or 
environmentally hazardous areas, or the presence of fire hazards, structural 
instability, unsanitary conditions posing disease risk, and Encampments located in 
flood zones or environmentally hazardous areas. 
 

2.3 “Encampments” means temporary or informal Structures on Publicly Owned Land 

established by one or more persons who are Unsheltered. 

 

2.4 “Outreach” means direct engagement by trained community-based service providers 

under contract with the City, working with individuals residing in Encampments to 
offer support, assess needs, provide information, connect them with housing, health, 
and social services, as well as acting as the first point of communication to warn 
those individuals of potential contraventions of City by-laws, relevant provincial 
legislation or regulation, or other legal issues that may relate to their use and 
occupation of Public Land; 

 

 

 

 

 

2.5 “Public Land” includes any municipal or provincially-owned real property, including 

sidewalks, lanes, streets, boulevards, roads, or street allowances, rights-of-way, 
vacant public land, parking lots, or parks.  
 

2.6 “Structures” means temporary or semi-permanent shelters, such as tents, tarps, 

shacks, vehicles, or other constructed or assembled enclosures, used by an 
individual or group for habitation in an outdoor setting. These structures are not 
designed for permanent human habitation and are typically situated on public or 
private land without formal authorization.  
 

2.7 “Unsheltered” means the conditions wherein a person or group lives in spaces not 

designed for human habitation, such as streets, vehicles, parks, or abandoned 
buildings. 

 

3.  The Policy  
 

3.1 Scope: This Policy applies to all City representatives who interact with 

Encampments. The Policy does not  impact Winnipeg Police Service (“WPS”) or 
Winnipeg Fire and Paramedic Service (“WFPS”)’s, response to calls for service, 
though WPS and WFPS may rely upon the same City resources referenced 
throughout this Policy in performing their high-priority emergent work. 

 

3.2 Guiding Principles: The following principles shall be adhered to in carrying out the 

Policy. 
 

3.2.1  Accountability and Transparency: The City will implement consistent 

procedures for decision-making, documentation, and reporting related to 
Encampment response activities. Regular updates on the implementation of 
the Policy and associated outcomes will be shared with City Council and the 
public. 

3.2.2  Health and Well-being: Recognizing that Encampments are not a safe or 

sustainable alternative to permanent housing—and that living in 
Encampments can pose serious risks to health, safety, and dignity—the City 
will balance Outreach, support, and enforcement measures to safeguard both 
individual and public well-being.  

3.2.3  Maintaining Public Wellbeing and Order: Under The City of Winnipeg 

Charter, the purposes of the City include developing and maintaining safe, 
orderly, viable and sustainable communities, and promoting and maintaining 
the health, safety, and welfare of its inhabitants. 

 

3.3 Council direction on Encampment enforcement and remediation measures: On 

September 25, 2025, City Council directed the Public Service to implement the 
following by November 17, 2025: 

 

 

 

 

 

3.3.1  Prohibit Encampments and prioritize enforcement in the following areas: 

A.  Within 50 metres of the perimeter of playgrounds, pools, spray pads, or 

outdoor recreational facilities designed for children, families, or seniors; 

B.  Within 50 metres of the perimeter of schools, daycares, or adult daycare 

facilities; 

C.  Where a hazard or obstruction to vehicular or pedestrian traffic exists; 
D.  Within a median or traffic island; 
E.  Within 30 metres of a transit shelter; 
F.  Within 30 metres of bridges, docks, or piers; 
G.  Within 50 metres of a rail line or crossing; 
H.  And where life safety issues exist; 

 

3.3.2  That the Public Service be directed to prioritize enforcement on all other City 

properties and spaces between the following times: 

A.  One hour after sunrise 
B.  One hour before sunset 

 

3.3.3  That the Public Service be directed to work with outreach service providers to 

give reasonable notice of the intended enforcement to Encampment residents 
and notify them of shelter options.  
 

3.3.4  That vacated Encampments be remediated as soon as possible to clear them 

of debris and detritus.  

 

3.3.5  That existing resources be allocated for enforcement and garbage 

remediation, through the Chief Administrative Office, as required.  

 

3.4 Encampment Response Protocol: Under the authority of the Chief Administrative 

Officer, the Public Service will develop and implement an Encampment response 
protocol that is informed by this Policy and reflects the evolving nature of Canada’s 
ongoing housing crisis. The Encampment Response Protocol will outline the 
following: 

 

3.4.1  How the City will prioritize Encampments for enforcement and Garbage 

remediation; 

3.4.2  How City departments and relevant external partners will operationalize the 

Policy, including how by-laws that relate to Encampments will be enforced;  

3.4.3  Procedures guiding the handling and storage or removal of personal property 

associated with Encampments;  

3.4.4  Inter-departmental Coordination: how departments will communicate with one 

another, who will lead decision-making respecting enforcement and 
remediation at Encampments, and how decisions will be actioned and 
documented. 

 

 

 

 

 

3.5 Responsibilities 

 

3.5.1  Chief Administrative Officer: Responsible for overseeing the development 

and implementation of the Encampment Response Protocol, serving as the 
City of Winnipeg lead at related cross-government or interdepartmental tables, 
and ensuring that proper procedure is followed by the Public Service when 
responding to and regulating Encampments. 

  

3.5.2  Community Development Division (CD): On behalf of the Chief 

Administrative Officer, CD will coordinate all actions and strategies associated 
with the Encampment Protocol, including chairing an inter-departmental 
Encampment Coordination Table, evaluating, reporting on and updating the 
Encampment Response Protocol as directed.  

 

3.5.3  Winnipeg Fire Paramedic Services (WFPS): Responsible for providing life 

safety information, educating Encampment residents on fire hazards, 
mitigating fire and life safety hazards in Encampments, mitigating fire and life 
safety risks, and issuing orders or taking immediate actions for the 
remediation of urgent or emergent fire life safety issues. 

 

3.5.4  Winnipeg Police Service (WPS): WPS will assist in documenting by-law 

violations along with other available City departments, and support evictions 
and cleanup by providing a presence to keep the peace.  

 

3.5.5  Legal Services: Will provide comprehensive legal advice on request, 

including identifying applicable legislation, legal precedent, and municipal by-
laws.  
 

3.5.6  All Other Departments: Responsible for adhering to the Policy and 

associated Protocol in all Encampment-related operations. 

 

3.5.7  Outreach: Outreach is responsible for adhering to the Policy and associated 

Protocol in all Encampment-related operations or as directed through relevant 
contracts and agreements.

APPENDIX B  – Encampment Response Protocol 
 

City of Winnipeg Encampment Response Protocol  

 

Updated October 27, 2025 

 

Summary 
 
The Encampment Response Protocol (“Protocol”) is the City of Winnipeg’s (“City”) operational 
guide to addressing non-emergency issues pertaining to encampments on public properties. It 
outlines procedures for engagement, assessment, and coordinated response to encampments 
and is guided by the City’s Encampment Policy and relevant by-laws. The Protocol is an 
operational plan under the authority of the Chief Administrative Officer; it will be updated 
regularly as processes are evaluated and issues relating to encampments evolve. 
 
The Protocol is not applicable to the regulation of encampments on private property or to 
situations that involve emergencies, life safety or criminal matters. Neither the Protocol nor the 
Encampment Policy impact the Winnipeg Police Service (WPS) or Winnipeg Fire and 
Paramedic Service (WFPS)’s response to calls for service.  
 
The Protocol is divided into four sections: 1) Coordination, 2) Three-level Prioritization, 3) 
The Response Process, and 4) Internal and External Responsibilities. 

 

1.  Coordination 

 
Management of the City’s operational response to issues relating to encampments will be 
the responsibility of an Encampment Coordination Table (ECT), reporting to the City’s Chief 
Administrative Officer. 
 
ECT membership will include the following City departments and divisions: WFPS; Public 
Works (PW); Planning, Property, and Development (PPD); Community Development (CD); 
WPS; Winnipeg Transit; and any other departments as required from time to time (e.g. Legal 
Services, Corporate Communications, Licensing and By-law Enforcement, Community 
Safety Officers).  
 
The general tasks of the ECT include: 

•  Meeting weekly or as necessary to consider encampment-related issues; 

•  Triaging and planning responses to encampment-related issues based on priority 

factors, as guided by a three-level prioritization approach (detailed below);

 

•  Coordinating on-the-ground encampment activities among relevant departments; 

•  Establishing procedures for documentation, evaluation, and reporting related to 

encampment response activities.

 

 
Three-Level Prioritization  
 
The City will employ a tiered approach to resource allocation in managing encampments on 
public property. This model prioritizes health and safety risks, proximity to sensitive public 
spaces, and the degree of community impact. Enforcement levels are guided by site 
characteristics, scale, and urgency of response. 
 

 

2 

 

Level 1 – Urgent Coordinated Response (High Risk / Sensitive Public Properties) 

 
Description: 
Encampments that pose an immediate risk to public safety, are located on or adjacent to 
sensitive public property, or obstruct essential services. 
 
Criteria include (and “Sensitive Public Property” defined as): 
•  Located within 50 metres of: 

o

  Playgrounds, pools, spray pads, or outdoor recreation facilities designed for 

children, families, or seniors; and  

o

  Schools, daycares, or personal care home facilities; 

•  Where a hazard or obstruction exists for vehicular, cycling, or pedestrian traffic; 

•  Located within medians, traffic islands, underpasses, or within 30 metres of Transit 

shelters, bridges, docks, or piers;  

•  Within 50 metres of a rail line or crossing; 

•  Sites where life safety concerns are present, such as: 

o

  Unsafe structures; 

o

  Active fire, flood or electrical hazards; 

o

  Floodplain, riverbank erosion, storm drain proximity); and 

•  Encampments causing service disruption to City operations or emergency access 

routes. 

 
Goals: Protect public safety, prevent harm, and ensure rapid restoration of sensitive or 
high-risk areas. 

 
Level 2 – Coordinated Response (Moderate Risk / Large Encampments on Non-Sensitive 
Properties) 

 
Description: 
Encampments on public property not classified as sensitive but that involve multiple 
occupants, significant debris, and other documented concerns. 
 
Criteria include: 

o

 

Encampments with permanent unpermitted structures;  

o

 

Evidence of illegal dumping, debris accumulation, or environmental contamination 
(e.g., biohazards, fuel spills, waste buildup); 

o

 

Signs of fire risk that require urgent action to mitigate but WFPS does not have 
scene control; 

o

 

Encampments interfering with park maintenance, trail access, or public use of open 
spaces; 

o

 

Presence of non-structural hazards, such as rodents, unsanitary waste, or used 
needles; 

o

 

Vulnerable occupants requiring case coordination (e.g., youth, seniors, persons with 
mobility limitations); and 

o

 

Repeated failure to comply with posted notices or outreach offers. 

 
Goals: Reduce health and safety risks, support relocation to safer alternatives, and 
restore public property. 

 
 

 

3 

 

Level 3 – Monitored Response (Low Risk / Small Encampments) 

 
Description: 
Small, low-impact encampments with minimal health, safety, or nuisance concerns 
located on non-sensitive public property. 
 
Criteria include: 

•  Small-scale encampments; 
•  Located on non-sensitive public property (e.g., open areas, park peripheries, 

non-operational rights-of-way); 

•  Minimal community complaints or evidence of nuisance activity; 
•  No visible hazards (no open flames, structural instability, or environmental 

degradation); 

•  No interference with City services, operations, or public access; and 
•  Outreach engagement ongoing and occupants are connected or willing to 

connect with supports. 

 
Goals: Ensuring ongoing safety and cleanliness of public property. 

 
The Response Process 
 
The following outlines the standard sequence of actions and coordination protocols for each 
level of encampment response. In certain instances the specific circumstances of an 
encampment will require that the following process is followed in different order. Note that 
immediate actions related to health, life safety, or criminal activity concerns are managed 
separately from this protocol. These situations often require urgent intervention and are 
addressed through existing practices. However, as the same resources may be involved, 
immediate life-safety responses can result in delays to other encampment response activities. 
 
Level One: 

 

1.  Identification 

o

 

Encampment identified through 311, inspection, or City staff observation; 

o

 

ECT assesses for risk (size, safety hazards, environmental impact) and

HISTORY/DISCUSSION 

 
The Director of the Community Services Department has the authority to waive or reduce fees 
pursuant to the Council-approved Community Services Department’s Recreation, Leisure and 
Library Fees and Charges Policy, which is exercised through the established Recreation 
Services Fee Assistance Program (“Program”). The Program provides eligible residents with 
discounted/waived facility and equipment access and leisure guide activity fees.  
 
Current clients of the provincial Employment and Income Assistance (EIA) program are eligible 
for the Recreation Services Fee Assistance Program. The recommended Information Sharing 
Agreement will facilitate the electronic confirmation of an individual’s eligibility, replacing a 
manual process with an automated system. The agreement creates efficiency for the exchange 
of personal information and improves the Program application process for all involved. 
  
The agreement ensures sharing of personal information between the two parties conforms with 
all applicable privacy and other legislative requirements. It also sets out in writing the conditions 
and procedures for sharing information, and for its use, protection and disclosure.  
 
Specifically, it addresses:  

•  The fields of data that will be shared 
•  Appropriate use of the information 
•  Confidentiality and protection of the information  
•  Appropriate disclosure of the information  

4 

•  Retention and destruction terms  
•  The complaint process  

 
The collection, use and safeguarding of the personal information collected is in accordance with 
the requirements outlined in The Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act (FIPPA).  
 
The agreement remains in place indefinitely until one party provides notice of the intent to 
terminate or in the event one party fails to comply with their obligations. 
 
Transit Services completed a similar information sharing agreement with the Province in 2020. 
Completing an Information Sharing Agreement between the Community Services Department 
and the Province provides efficiency between the three parties to provide fee assistance support 
to eligible applicants in one intake. 
 
There are no financial considerations for either party as part of this agreement.

There are no direct financial or other implications resulting from the recommendations of this 
report. The values of fee assistance provided to individuals, groups and organizations are 
subject to limits established in the Department’s annual operating budget.  
 
Where fee subsidies are provided, revenue is offset by an amount equal to the revenue for the 
service provided based on the applicable fees and charges.  Potential loss to revenue from the 
provision of fee assistance is estimated to be minimal as the facility would likely not be used by 
the individual or organization or would not attend the recreation program if the fee assistance 
was not provided. 
 

HISTORY/DISCUSSION 

 
On October 24, 2007, Council approved the establishment of a Community Services 
Department Fees and Charges Policy (the Policy) to provide the framework and principles for 
the setting and administration of fees and charges for applicable recreation, leisure and library 
services, including criteria for fee assistance. While Library Service provides facility rental fee 

5 

waivers, it is the Recreation fee subsidies and waivers that are the majority of the waivers to 
citizens of Winnipeg because most of the library service is free.  
  
Recreation fee subsidies target low-income families and individuals that are residents of 
Winnipeg to provide equitable access to recreation/aquatics facilities and programs. Facility 
admission and/or program fees are either waived or reduced.  Eligibility is based on Statistics 
Canada Low Income Cut-Off guidelines and applicants are required to provide verification (i.e., 
social assistance case number, refugee assistance case number, Canada Revenue Agency 
Notice of Assessment, or statement of income from CPP Disability). The Recreation Fee 
Assistance Program is explained on page 5 of the Fall 2024 Winter 2025 Leisure Guide. 
 
On April 27, 2023, Council approved an update to the Policy and adopted the Community 
Services Department Recreation, Leisure and Library Fees and Charges Policy No. LW-009 
(the “Policy”). The Policy continues to provide criteria for fee assistance with two significant 
changes from the 2007 Policy: 
 
•  The Department is to report annually to the Standing Policy Committee - Community 

Services with a list of groups or organizations in receipt of fee waivers, including the amount 
of fees waived, for the previous year where the value of the waiver exceeds $5,000 and a 
total for all fee subsidies provided. 

•  The Director of Community Services is provided with authority to waive or reduce fees to 

non-profit groups or organizations and public schools that provide services that align with 
and support Council-adopted plans, policies, and strategies. However, as currently written, 
this authority is only available where the organization’s use of a department's facility or 
equipment is for a period of less than one year. 

 
On February 22, 2024, Council approved an amendment to the Policy with key changes which 
include: 
 
•  All non-resident fees for recreation and library services reflect a surcharge of an additional 

100 percent of the resident fee.  

•  A Policy amendment to include government agencies in the list of organizations that the 

Director of Community Services can waive or reduce fees if the services of the agency and 
the request for fee reduction meet all the criteria of the Policy. 

 
These updates to the Policy were intended to increase the transparency regarding fee 
assistance provided by the Department through an annual report, and to ensure Council 
supports continued fee subsidies and waivers to groups and organizations that wish to access 
Community Services facilities to deliver their programs and services.

Policy Amendment 
 
Prior to the 2023 Policy update, the Director of Community Services could approve a fee 
subsidy or waiver for any length of time to a group or organization that met the criteria of the 
Policy. Many groups and organizations have received subsidies or waivers for years to deliver 
their community-based services in Community Services facilities. The intent of the 2023 Policy 
update was to provide transparency relative to the Department’s fee waivers and approve 
longer term facility use by such organizations. However, the language in the update 
unintentionally limited organizations’ ability to apply for and receive fee subsidies or waivers 
each year. 
 
The proposed policy amendment clarifies that subsidies are subject to the Department’s annual 
budget and will remain subject to approval on an annual basis but will remove the restriction on 
organizations from being approved for a subsidy in more than one year. 
 
This change will allow Community Services to continue to determine how to best provide 
ongoing support to these organizations while also providing certainty that these organizations 
will have available facilities to deliver their services.

HISTORY/DISCUSSION 

 
Kinsmen Sherbrook Pool Fitness Centre 

Located on the second floor above the entrance and office space, the rooms were not designed 
as a fitness centre. It provides an adequate area for treadmills and a limited selection of 
resistance machines. The gym is clean and well-maintained, but physical limitations such as 
small pedestrian doors, narrow stairs, and no elevator, present barriers to accessibility. 
 
The cardio equipment is dated and requires replacement. The plate-resistance machines are 
old but functional. Air-resistance training machines are dated, noisy and complicated for users. 
Three new pieces of cardio equipment are expected to be installed by the end of 2025. The 
tender for replacement of resistance machines is expected to be awarded in early 2026. The 
narrow pedestrian doors and lack of an elevator create a considerable challenge to install new 
machines. 
 
Revenue and Sustainability 
 
In 2024, admission sales for adult drop-in revenue were approximately $7,175. With the current 
system, the Public Service cannot say which daily admission sales were for pool only, fitness 
centre only, or for the entire facility. 
 
The Winnipeg Recreation Strategy refers to closing aged infrastructure and investing in new 
regional recreation centres. Although Kinsmen Sherbrook Pool is the City’s oldest swimming 
pool, it provides a unique service in an area of high need. 
 
Simplifying fees and charges is a strategic goal to support citizens. Stand-alone fees for specific 
centres complicates fees and charges and can confuse users. Most membership rates currently 
provide access to all City-owned and operated facilities and services. 
 
Fee Assistance Program 
 
The City of Winnipeg offers subsidized Recreation Services facilities and programs. 
Winnipeggers who experience financial barriers to participation may benefit from the City’s 
Recreation Fee Assistance Program. The fee assistance program gives applicants a renewable 
10-visit pass to recreation facilities or a leisure guide program credit. These are provided at no 
charge to residents whose income meets the program criteria. 
 
Two Year Pilot 
Under the Community Services Department Recreation, Leisure and Library Fees and Charges 
Policy LW-009, the Director of Community Services has the authority to establish and set 
recreation fees and charges including public admissions to facilities.

The Greenspace Plan will establish the planning and land management priorities for 
greenspaces and natural corridors in Winnipeg. As a by-law, the plan will ensure natural areas 
and features are properly valued, protected and cared for, for the benefit of all living things, now 
and in the future. 
 
Complete Communities By-law No. 119/2020, approved in May 2022, also directs the work and

On July 13, 2023, Council endorsed the signing on to the Montreal Pledge. The Montreal 
Pledge is a global biodiversity initiative launched at COP15—the Fifteenth convention on 
Biological Diversity—challenging cities to commit to 15 concrete actions that protect 
ecosystems, reduce threats to biodiversity, and promote inclusive governance and education. 
The overarching goal and global commitment of this initiative is to halt the loss of biodiversity 
and preserve 30 percent of lands and oceans by 2030. The Biodiversity Policy will help 
operationalize the City’s commitment to this pledge by guiding the protection, enhancement and 
proper management of greenspaces and natural areas under the City’s care, custody and 
control. 
 
The 2022 Capital Budget included $700,000 for the creation of a Master Green Space and 
Natural Corridors Plan By-law, financed, in part, by the Canada Community-Building Fund. 
 
A preliminary research and project planning phase was undertaken. This included consultant

Why are greenspaces and biodiversity important for Winnipeg?

 

Biodiversity is vital. It supports healthy ecosystems that provide clean air 
and water, food, shelter, and climate regulation. Biodiversity also helps 
ecosystems recover from and adapt to climate change. Urban 
greenspaces like parks, rivers, and tree-lined streets play a key role in 
cities. They provide habitats and link natural areas to support 
biodiversity and promote the health and wellness of all living things.  

Through a deep sense of kinship and respect for nature, Indigenous 
knowledge systems have sustained biodiversity for generations. Both 
Western and Indigenous science show that access to quality natural 
spaces improves physical and mental health, strengthens communities, 
and enhances overall well-being. The City plays an important role in 
protecting these areas and making sure everyone has fair access to them 
now and in the future.  

Biodiversity is at risk worldwide, with species currently going extinct tens 
to hundreds of times faster than in the past. The main threats to 
biodiversity in Winnipeg include: 

Habitat loss and degradation: both urbanization and disruption of 
natural processes are some of the threats to healthy ecosystems. 

Invasive alien species: the impact of plants, animals and diseases that 
can outcompete native populations and degrade their habitat. Invasive 
species can gain a foothold when ecosystems are degraded, and when 
climate change creates more favourable conditions for non-native 
species to thrive. 

Pollution: harms species directly and damages their habitats, which can 
change the health and presence of plants and animals in an ecosystem. 
For example, when nutrient-rich runoﬀ flows into rivers, it can cause 
algae blooms downstream. 

Climate change: aﬀects biodiversity by altering the conditions that 
plants and animals need to survive. These changes can lead to impacts 
such as habitat loss, shifting when flowers bloom, and disruptions to 
migration patterns. All of these changes undermine the natural balance 
of ecosystems. 

In response to these threats, international agencies and national 
governments have agreed to commitments to improve protections and 
restore ecosystems around the world.  

The Montreal Pledge—On July 13, 2023, the City of Winnipeg (City) 
Council signed the Montreal Pledge. By signing the pledge, the City 
agrees to implement the 15 actions of the pledge. This commits the City 
to protect and restore biodiversity through governance, partnership, 
management, and education. Actions range in scale and scope from 
achieving regional goals to highly specific targets. Many of the policies 
within the Greenspace Plan and the Biodiversity Policy align with this 
initiative.

An overview of Winnipeg’s natural heritage 

Winnipeg's location, at the confluence of the Red and Assiniboine 
rivers, is fundamental to its history, development, and identity. The 
rivers have shaped the city's geography, transportation routes, and 
cultural heritage from time immemorial. 

For thousands of years, Indigenous people have had respectful, two-
way relationships with the land we now call Winnipeg. These 
relationships are guided by values such as care, ceremony, and 
responsibility. They are rooted in teachings such as the Seven 
Generations philosophy and the worldview that land is not a 
possession, but a living relative to be respected and cared for. 

Watersheds—Watersheds are areas where all surface water (rain, snow 
melt, etc.) drains into a common body of water, such as rivers, creeks, 
lakes, etc.  Winnipeg is located within the Lake Winnipeg Basin, which 
includes major rivers such as the Red, Assiniboine, and the Winnipeg 
rivers.  Water flows into the watersheds within the City from the south, 
originating in North Dakota and Minnesota, and from the west, 
traveling through Alberta and Saskatchewan. All of this water then 
flows into the Hudson Bay drainage basin. These water connections are 
vital for fish and bird migration, nutrient cycling and seasonal flow 
regulation. 

There are five sub-watersheds in the Winnipeg area that form part of 
the Lake Winnipeg Basin: the La Salle River; the Seine River; the Lower 
Assiniboine; the Cooks Creek/Devils Creek; and the Netley 
Creek/Grassmere Creek sub-watersheds (Figure 2).  

These river systems form some of Winnipeg’s most significant natural 
corridors and have supported food, medicine and travel since time 
immemorial. 

Vegetation & Ecology—Winnipeg is located within the northern extent 
of the Tall-grass Prairie ecosystem, which historically stretched from 
Winnipeg south to the state of Texas. One hundred and fifty years ago, 
large herds of bison, elk, and antelope ranged across the Tall-grass 
Prairie, which helped to maintain the ecosystem through grazing and 
other activity such as bison “wallows”.  Wallows are made when the 
animals roll on the ground, create depressions that gather rainwater, 
which in turn provide habitat for other plants and animals. The 
ecosystem was also managed by Indigenous peoples through practices 
such as controlled burns. Today, less than one percent of the original 
Tall-grass Prairie ecosystem remains. It is now considered one of the 
most endangered ecosystems in the world.

continuity, climate adaptation, water quality and flood control, 
pollination and seed dispersal, and human health and wellness.

Indigenous Knowledge and Stewardship 

First Nations, Inuit and Métis Peoples in the Winnipeg area each hold 
distinct understandings of the world—how it came to be, and how to 
stay in proper relationship with it.   

Indigenous Peoples have lived in and with these lands for thousands of 
years before the arrival of European settlers and their governance 
systems. Their communities have distinct governance systems, often 
rooted in kinship, oral traditions, and reciprocal relationships with the 
land. They have established diverse systems to organize people and 
resources and actively shaped the landscape through traditional 
stewardship practices. 

Indigenous laws, governance, and stewardship practices vary across 
and within Indigenous cultures, and include a wide range of practices 
to actively influence the natural environment. Pushing back against 
stereotypes of pre-contact people as passive hunters and gatherers, 
Anishinaabe, Ininew, Dakota and Métis researchers and scientists have 
documented numerous historical stewardship practices relevant to this 
region—practices that have been adapted and adopted in current land 
stewardship, including: 

•

  Managing the movement of bison and improving the soil 

through controlled burns 

•

  Sustainable harvesting of plants and animals 

•

  Agricultural practices, such as corn and wild rice cultivation 

•

  Forest management and berry production through controlled 

burns

 

Indigenous Knowledge is dynamic and adaptive and continues to 
evolve through engagement with new tools and challenges such as 
climate change, and the impacts of technology and urbanization. 

Systems Thinking and Planning Through Indigenous Lenses—
Indigenous knowledge systems are built on strong relationships and 
the idea that everything in nature is connected. They pay attention to 
the relationships between water, soil, animals, seasons, and ceremony. 
This systems-based view is well-suited to address complex challenges 
such as climate change and biodiversity loss. Indigenous science is 
place-based, grounded in observation, intergenerational knowledge 
and accountability to all living things—strengthened through 
storytelling, and lived experience. 

Braiding Knowledge—Two-Eyed Seeing as a guiding approach calls for 
equal respect and attention to both Indigenous and Western ways of 
knowing. Indigenous worldviews include spirit, ceremony, and the 
understanding that everything in nature is connected. These elements 
are often overlooked or misunderstood within Western frameworks. 
Western science, on the other hand, tends to turn things into numbers 
so they can be measured or counted and break things down into 
smaller, simpler parts to try to understand it, versus looking at the 
whole.  This can contrast sharply with the holistic nature of Indigenous 
perspectives. Co-creating a plan that draws from both knowledge

Rethinking Infrastructure 

The City is structured around networks of natural and engineered 
infrastructure. These networks deliver the goods and services required 
for a modern urban economy to function. Conventional ‘grey’ 
infrastructure (such as pipes, culverts and roads) is typically used to 

deliver essential services like clean drinking water, stormwater 
management and flood mitigation. These technologies often result in 
environmental and economic impacts including greenhouse gas 
emissions, high embedded energy consumption, depreciation of 
investments, and the erosion and destruction of natural areas.   

In recent years, many municipal asset managers and climate scientists 
have pushed to reframe natural areas and greenspaces as a form of 
“green infrastructure” that provide ecological, social and economic 
benefits in urban areas. Unlike grey infrastructure, green infrastructure 
uses vegetation, soils and natural processes to deliver services in a 
more sustainable and resilient way.   

Green Infrastructure—Green infrastructure includes both natural 
areas and built vegetated features that mimic natural processes to 
provide environmental, social and economic services. This type of 
infrastructure provides many benefits by managing essential services 
like water, air quality, and temperature, while also oﬀering spaces for 
recreation and enhancing biodiversity. Green infrastructure, whether 
natural assets like forests and wetlands or enhanced assets like rain 
gardens, uses biological processes to do some of the work typically 
performed by mechanical or ‘grey’ infrastructure.  

Green infrastructure examples: 

•

  Trees and urban forests 

•

  Parks and open spaces 

•

  Wetlands and river corridors 

•

  Green roofs and walls 

•

  Bioswales and rain gardens

“Cultural Infrastructure” (CI) is an important but often overlooked 
dimension of greenspace planning. In the context of Indigenous-led 
approaches, CI represents the deep interconnection between land, 
governance, knowledge systems, and community well-being. Unlike 
conventional infrastructure, which often separates building from 
nature, CI recognizes human-built systems as part of living 
landscapes—connected with ceremony, stewardship, food systems, 
governance, and cultural identity. 

Cultural Infrastructure can be defined as human-built systems and 
structures deeply intertwined with cultural practices, Indigenous 
governance, and ecological stewardship. These systems foster the 
health and well-being of both communities and the land, acting as 
sophisticated nature-based solutions that protect and regenerate 

biodiversity. This understanding is foundational to the success of 
Winnipeg’s Greenspace Plan and Biodiversity Policy. It bridges Western 
perspectives and research and Indigenous knowledge and sciences, in 
the interest of ensuring that land-based interventions are grounded in 
place-based knowledge, reciprocal relations, and sustainability. This is 
fundamental to protecting and enhancing biodiversity and to 
embedding resiliency in city decision-making.  

Indigenous knowledge systems position resilience as a relational 
process, deeply rooted in reciprocal relationships between people, 
land, water, and all living beings. To eﬀectively integrate this 
knowledge into municipal planning and decision-making, the City must 
prioritize the restoration of cultural landscapes—protecting and 
restoring spaces where traditional harvesting, teaching and 
governance practices are actively sustained.

Ecosystem Services Valuation  

Nature located within the city provides many benefits to residents, 
which are often undervalued. These are life sustaining benefits such as: 
the control of the flow, storage and quality of water; cleaning the air; 
capturing and storing carbon dioxide from the air; and providing a 
home and food for animals, plants and other living things. Even though 
these ecosystem services are absolutely needed for both ecological and 
human well-being, they are rarely included in policy or investment 
decisions.  

Value methodology—Ecosystem service valuation is a method used in 
Western science that uses money-based methods to determine how 
much the benefits we get from nature, such as clean water, fresh air, 
and recreation, are worth.  These include both direct benefits (like 
building energy savings from tree canopy) and indirect benefits (such 
as improved water quality from wetlands). 

Putting a value to the benefits of nature is a way to make invisible 
benefits visible and helps with decision making.  This oﬀers a new way 
of thinking to help guide city planning, biodiversity protection, and 
making smart investments in green infrastructure.   

This valuation oﬀers a conservative estimate of the value of nature in 
Winnipeg. It focuses on those ecosystem services that can be measured 
with available data and established economic methods.

  Address Land Constraints Through Regional Connectivity: With 

only 7.2% of Winnipeg’s area remaining in a natural condition 
strengthening connections to regional natural areas—such as river 
corridors, wetlands, and forest patches outside municipal limits—

can help support biodiversity and ecological function at the 
landscape scale. 

•

  Invest in Wetland Conservation: Wetlands are among the highest-

value ecosystems per hectare. Protecting and restoring wetlands 
can yield significant returns in water quality, flood resilience, and 
biodiversity. 

•

  Expand and Maintain Urban Tree Canopy: Canopy cover delivers 

climate and health benefits. Tree planting, maintenance, and 
protection of existing canopy should be embedded in city planning. 

•

  Integrate Natural Assets in Planning: Green infrastructure can 

complement grey infrastructure. Accounting for ecosystem services 
in capital planning and asset management enhances resilience and 
cost-eﬀectiveness. 

•

  Advance Biodiversity Protection: Prairies, forests, and riparian 

areas provide vital habitat. These should be prioritized in land-use 
decisions and connected through ecological corridors. 

•

  Support Community Engagement: Awareness and stewardship of 

nature are critical. Programs that involve residents in tree planting, 
habitat restoration, and monitoring can amplify benefits. 

•

  Use Valuation to Help Prioritize Investments: Ecosystem service 

values can help compare the relative benefits of alternative 
investments in natural infrastructure. This can support cost-benefit 
analysis, budget justification, and the strategic use of limited 
conservation resources.

Key Considerations  
 
Protection 
Observations & Issues 

•

  Current policies and regulations provide limited protection for 

trees and natural areas on private lands, leaving many ecologically 
valuable sites vulnerable to development or degradation 

•

  Existing municipal by-laws, including those governing waterways 

and tree preservation, are not fully aligned with biodiversity 
objectives and do not explicitly address sensitive habitats outside 
of designated parks or flood zones 

 

Potential directions 

•

  Policy Integration: Incorporate biodiversity considerations 

into all land use planning and infrastructure decisions, 
including additional weighting and consideration in the City’s 
asset management and budget processes (business cases, 
multi-criteria prioritization) 

•

  By-law Enhancement: Strengthen by-laws to provide explicit 

protection for upland and ecologically sensitive areas beyond 
waterway corridors. The Zoning By-law, which controls 
activities on private lands, may be key to this. 

•

  Permitting Reform: Align permitting processes with 

biodiversity goals by requiring ecological assessments and 
restoration conditions where natural areas are impacted. 

•

  Buﬀer Zones: Establish buﬀer zones around wetlands, riparian 

areas, and other sensitive natural areas to safeguard 
biodiversity. 

•

  Data-Driven Planning: Use updated ecological mapping and 

site assessments to identify high-priority areas for protection 
and ensure these are reflected in municipal decision-making. 

•

  Awareness Building: Engage landowners, developers, and 

residents in understanding the importance of local biodiversity 
and the roles they can play in conservation. 

 
Acquisition 
Observations & Issues 

•

  Winnipeg currently lacks a dedicated and sustained capital budget 

for strategic greenspace acquisition 

•

  Parkland dedication through development processes has not kept 

pace with the need to secure ecologically significant lands, and the 
Land Dedication Reserve Fund (LDR) has been limited in scope and 
impact 

•

  In many areas, acquisition is reactive rather than proactive, 

resulting in missed opportunities to protect key natural areas 

Potential directions 

•

  Dedicated Funding: Establish a dedicated acquisition fund to 

proactively secure lands identified as high priority for biodiversity 
protection, corridor connectivity, and improvements to equitable 
access. 

•

  Strategic Targeting: Prioritize acquisitions that fill geographic or 

ecological gaps in the city’s greenspace network, particularly in 
under-served communities or ecologically fragmented areas. 

•

  Innovative Financial Tools: Utilize tools such as development 

levies, density bonuses, or conservation easements to support 
acquisition objectives.

•

  Collaborative Acquisition: Explore opportunities for collaborative

•

  Policy Alignment: Integrate acquisition priorities into broader

Restoration 
Observations & Issues 

•

  Restoration of natural areas requires sustained financial 

investment and institutional capacity. 

•

  Existing restoration eﬀorts, though commendable, are often 

limited in scope and constrained by competing operational 
priorities. 

•

  The ecological integrity of many areas continues to be threatened 

by invasive species, fragmentation, and altered hydrology 
resulting from urban development and climate change. 

Potential directions 

•

  Habitat Restoration: Prioritize the restoration of degraded 

riparian corridors and wetlands, prairie remnants, and urban 
forest patches. Apply the principles of the “restorative continuum” 
to tailor the form and intensity   eﬀorts to the situation. 

•

  Connectivity Repair: Implement restoration strategies that 

reconnect fragmented habitats, drawing on historical ecological 
patterns and current land use contexts. 

•

  Reduce Urban Impacts: Use legislation to address urban wildlife 

stressors such as light pollution and domestic pet predation. 

•

  Community Stewardship: Support community-led restoration 

initiatives, including Indigenous land stewardship projects and 
citizen science eﬀorts. 

•

  Long-Term Planning: Establish long-term restoration objectives 

with measurable targets to track progress and guide adaptive 
management.

Observations & Issues 

•

  There is a widespread misconception that natural areas require 

little or no ongoing maintenance. In reality achieving long-term 
ecological resilience in urban natural areas requires active, 
adaptive management. 

•

  Current budgets and staﬀing levels limit the City’s ability to 

provide consistent care to these spaces, undermining their long-
term ecological function. 

•

  The management of some assets like roadside bioswales overlaps 

several City departments, creating jurisdictional challenges. 

•

  Some maintenance activities such as prescribed burns would 

benefit from public information

  campaigns.

restoration, invasive species control, and ecological maintenance. 

•

  Ecological Practices: Expand the use of ecologically appropriate 

maintenance techniques, including prescribed burns, selective 
mowing, and targeted removals of invasive species.

Education

•

  Misunderstandings and cultural biases about the value and 

appearance of natural landscapes in the city continue to influence 
public opinion and decision-making. 

•

  Many residents and stakeholders equate ecological complexity with 

neglect, creating resistance to eﬀorts aimed at naturalization and 
restoration. 

Potential directions 

•

  Public Communications: Develop communications strategies to 

highlight the role of natural areas in climate adaptation, biodiversity 
protection, and human wellbeing. 

•

  Interpretive Programming: Create interpretive signage and 

educational programming to support understanding of native 
ecosystems. 

•

  Sharing Indigenous Knowledge: Support programming aimed at 

sharing Indigenous perspectives of the land and our place within it. 

•

  Cultural Shift: Promote a cultural shift in perceptions of beauty and 

maintenance in public greenspaces through demonstration projects 
and storytelling. 

•

  Youth Engagement: Engage youth and schools in stewardship and 

monitoring activities to foster early connections to nature. 

 

Partnerships 

Observations & Issues 

•

  Competing policy goals across municipal departments and 

external agencies can create tensions that limit greenspace 
outcomes. 

•

  While there is strong community interest in stewardship, 

coordination between community-based groups, Indigenous 
Nations, and municipal bodies remains inconsistent.

  Interdepartmental Alignment: Formalize interdepartmental

ensure alignment across transportation, planning, infrastructure, 
and sustainability mandates. 

•

  Indigenous Leadership: Strengthen partnerships with Indigenous 

Nations to support land governance and stewardship in 
greenspace planning and management. 

•

  Community Capacity: Support the capacity of community 

stewardship organizations through long-term funding and 
collaborative frameworks. 

•

  Shared Stewardship: Build shared ownership of biodiversity 

goals by working with local businesses, institutions, and residents 
on greening and monitoring initiatives.

Monitoring 

Observations & Issues 

•

  Long-term monitoring of ecological health is not systematically 

embedded into Winnipeg’s greenspace management frameworks. 

•

  Without regular data collection and evaluation, it is diﬀicult to 

assess the eﬀectiveness of restoration eﬀorts, adapt to changing 
conditions, or communicate progress to the public and partners. 

Potential directions 

•

  Monitoring Framework: Establish a city-wide ecological 

monitoring program focused on biodiversity indicators, habitat 
quality, and ecosystem services. 

•

  Remote Sensing: Use remote sensing technologies (e.g., NDVI) 

and GIS to track vegetation health, canopy cover, and connectivity 
over time. 

•

  Participatory Monitoring: Partner with community groups and 

Indigenous knowledge holders to support participatory 
monitoring and traditional ecological knowledge integration. 
These activities can be celebratory and educational as well. 

•

  Transparent Reporting: Report publicly on key metrics and 

trends to support transparency, learning, and accountability.
